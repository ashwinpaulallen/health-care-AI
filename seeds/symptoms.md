## Common Digestive Symptoms

### Bloating

**What is bloating?**
Bloating is a feeling of fullness, tightness, or swelling in the abdomen. It may be accompanied by visible distension (stomach looks larger) and discomfort.

**Common causes:**
- Eating too quickly and swallowing air
- Consuming gas-producing foods (beans, lentils, broccoli, cabbage, onions, carbonated drinks)
- Food intolerances (lactose, fructose, gluten sensitivity)
- Eating large meals
- Constipation
- Changes in gut bacteria
- High sodium intake causing water retention
- Menstrual cycle fluctuations

**General management strategies:**
- Eat slowly and chew thoroughly
- Avoid carbonated beverages
- Limit high-FODMAP foods if sensitive
- Stay hydrated with water
- Include regular physical activity
- Consider keeping a food diary to identify triggers
- Eat smaller, more frequent meals
- Reduce sodium intake
- Include probiotic-rich foods (yogurt, kefir) if tolerated

**When to seek medical attention:**
- Severe or persistent bloating lasting more than 2 weeks
- Bloating with unexplained weight loss
- Severe abdominal pain
- Blood in stool
- Persistent changes in bowel habits
- Fever
- Vomiting

### Dizziness

**What is dizziness?**
Dizziness can include feelings of lightheadedness, unsteadiness, vertigo (spinning sensation), or feeling faint.

**Common nutrition-related causes:**
- Dehydration (insufficient fluid intake)
- Low blood sugar (hypoglycemia) from skipping meals or inadequate carbohydrate intake
- Iron deficiency anemia
- Vitamin B12 deficiency
- Low blood pressure from sudden position changes (orthostatic hypotension)
- Electrolyte imbalances
- Excessive caffeine intake

**General management strategies:**
- Maintain adequate hydration (8-10 glasses of water daily)
- Eat regular, balanced meals every 3-4 hours
- Include iron-rich foods (leafy greens, legumes, fortified cereals) with vitamin C sources
- Ensure adequate protein intake
- Stand up slowly from sitting or lying positions
- Limit caffeine and alcohol
- Include complex carbohydrates for stable blood sugar

**When to seek immediate medical attention:**
- Dizziness with chest pain or difficulty breathing
- Sudden severe headache with dizziness
- Dizziness after head injury
- Loss of consciousness or fainting
- Confusion or slurred speech
- Numbness or weakness on one side of body
- Persistent dizziness lasting more than a few days
- Dizziness with hearing loss or ringing in ears

### Headaches

**Types of nutrition-related headaches:**

**Dehydration headaches:**
- Caused by insufficient fluid intake
- Often presents as dull, throbbing pain
- May worsen with movement
- Accompanied by thirst, dry mouth, dark urine

**Management:**
- Drink water regularly throughout the day
- Monitor urine color (should be pale yellow)
- Increase intake during hot weather or exercise
- Include water-rich foods (cucumber, watermelon, oranges)

**Low blood sugar headaches:**
- Triggered by skipping meals or prolonged fasting
- May include shakiness, irritability, difficulty concentrating
- Common in mid-morning or late afternoon

**Management:**
- Eat regular meals with complex carbohydrates
- Include protein and healthy fats for sustained energy
- Keep healthy snacks available (nuts, fruits, whole grain crackers)
- Avoid excessive refined sugar

**Caffeine-related headaches:**
- Can occur from excessive intake or sudden withdrawal
- Often presents as throbbing pain

**Management:**
- Gradually reduce caffeine if consuming excessively (>400mg/day)
- Avoid abrupt cessation
- Stay consistent with caffeine intake timing

**Food trigger headaches (in sensitive individuals):**
- Tyramine-containing foods (aged cheese, processed meats, fermented foods)
- MSG (monosodium glutamate)
- Artificial sweeteners
- Alcohol (especially red wine)
- Nitrates/nitrites in processed meats

**Management:**
- Keep a headache and food diary
- Identify and avoid personal triggers
- Read food labels carefully

**When to seek immediate medical attention:**
- Sudden, severe "thunderclap" headache
- Headache with fever, stiff neck, confusion, or vision changes
- Headache after head injury
- New headache pattern after age 50
- Headache with weakness, numbness, or difficulty speaking
- Progressive headache that worsens over days/weeks

### Dehydration

**What is dehydration?**
Dehydration occurs when your body loses more fluid than you take in, preventing normal bodily functions.

**Signs and symptoms:**
- Thirst
- Dry mouth and lips
- Dark yellow urine or decreased urination
- Fatigue and weakness
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Headache
- Dry skin
- Rapid heartbeat

**Severe dehydration signs (requires immediate medical attention):**
- Very dark urine or no urination for 8+ hours
- Extreme thirst
- Confusion or irritability
- Sunken eyes
- Rapid breathing
- Fainting

**Common causes:**
- Insufficient fluid intake
- Hot weather and excessive sweating
- Physical activity without adequate fluid replacement
- Illness with vomiting or diarrhea
- High fever
- Excessive urination (diabetes, diuretics)
- Alcohol consumption

**Prevention and management:**
- Drink at least 8-10 glasses (2-2.5 liters) of water daily
- Increase intake during hot weather, exercise, or illness
- Include hydrating foods (fruits, vegetables, soups, dal)
- Monitor urine color (pale yellow indicates good hydration)
- Limit diuretic beverages (coffee, tea, alcohol) or offset with extra water
- Include electrolyte-containing fluids if sweating heavily (coconut water, nimbu paani)

**Hydration tips for Indian climate:**
- Start the day with water or nimbu paani
- Include buttermilk (chaas), coconut water, or lassi
- Eat water-rich fruits (watermelon, muskmelon, oranges, cucumber)
- Have soups, dal, or rasam with meals
- Keep a water bottle accessible throughout the day

### GERD (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease) Basics

**What is GERD?**
GERD occurs when stomach acid frequently flows back into the esophagus, causing irritation and discomfort.

**Common symptoms:**
- Heartburn (burning sensation in chest)
- Acid regurgitation (sour or bitter taste)
- Difficulty swallowing
- Feeling of lump in throat
- Chronic cough or throat clearing
- Hoarse voice
- Chest pain (always rule out cardiac causes first)

**Common dietary triggers:**
- Spicy foods
- Fried and fatty foods
- Citrus fruits and juices
- Tomato-based foods
- Chocolate
- Mint
- Coffee and caffeinated beverages
- Carbonated drinks
- Alcohol
- Onions and garlic (in some people)

**General dietary management:**
- Eat smaller, more frequent meals
- Avoid eating 2-3 hours before bedtime
- Stay upright for 30-60 minutes after eating
- Identify and avoid personal trigger foods
- Limit fatty and fried foods
- Reduce spicy food intake if it triggers symptoms
- Avoid tight clothing around waist
- Maintain a healthy weight
- Elevate head of bed 6-8 inches

**Foods generally better tolerated:**
- Oatmeal and whole grains
- Non-citrus fruits (banana, melon, apple)
- Vegetables (except tomatoes, onions, garlic if triggers)
- Lean proteins (chicken, fish, legumes)
- Low-fat dairy or alternatives
- Ginger
- Whole wheat roti
- Plain rice
- Boiled or steamed preparations

**Indian meal modifications:**
- Choose boiled, steamed, or baked over fried
- Reduce oil/ghee in cooking
- Use mild spices; avoid excessive chili
- Opt for buttermilk over lassi
- Choose plain dal over spicy curries
- Avoid pickles, chutneys with excessive spice
- Include cooling foods (cucumber raita, coconut)

**When to seek medical attention:**
- Frequent heartburn (more than 2 times per week)
- Symptoms interfering with daily life
- Difficulty or pain when swallowing
- Persistent nausea or vomiting
- Unexplained weight loss
- Chest pain (always rule out heart issues first - seek emergency care if uncertain)
- Symptoms not improving with dietary changes

## Red Flags: When to Seek Immediate Medical Care

**Call emergency services or go to the emergency room immediately if you experience:**

1. **Chest pain** - especially with shortness of breath, sweating, nausea, or pain radiating to arm/jaw
2. **Severe abdominal pain** - especially if sudden onset or accompanied by fever
3. **Difficulty breathing** or shortness of breath
4. **Signs of severe dehydration** - confusion, no urination, rapid heartbeat, fainting
5. **Severe dizziness with:**
   - Chest pain
   - Head injury
   - Loss of consciousness
   - Vision changes
   - Slurred speech
   - Weakness or numbness
6. **Blood in vomit or stool** (red or black, tarry stool)
7. **Sudden, severe headache** unlike any previous headache
8. **Persistent vomiting** preventing fluid intake
9. **High fever with stiff neck** or confusion
10. **Sudden unexplained weight loss** (>5% body weight in a month)
11. **Severe allergic reaction** - difficulty breathing, swelling of face/throat, hives

## General Wellness Tips

**Balanced nutrition foundation:**
- Include variety: vegetables, fruits, whole grains, proteins, healthy fats
- Stay hydrated throughout the day
- Eat regular meals; don't skip breakfast
- Control portion sizes
- Limit processed foods, excessive sugar, and salt
- Listen to your body's hunger and fullness cues

**Lifestyle factors:**
- Regular physical activity (at least 30 minutes most days)
- Adequate sleep (7-9 hours)
- Stress management techniques
- Avoid tobacco
- Limit alcohol consumption

**When to consult a healthcare provider:**
- Persistent or worsening symptoms
- Symptoms affecting quality of life
- Unexplained changes in weight, appetite, or bowel habits
- Before starting any significant dietary changes
- For personalized nutrition advice
- If you have existing medical conditions
