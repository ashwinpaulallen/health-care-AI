# Food & Nutrition Reference Guide

A practical guide to understanding nutrients, making balanced food choices, and navigating common Indian dishes for better health.

---

## Core Nutrients Overview

### Protein

**What is protein?**
Protein is essential for building and repairing tissues, making enzymes and hormones, supporting immune function, and maintaining muscle mass.

**Daily needs:**
- Adults: approximately 0.8-1.0 grams per kg of body weight
- Active individuals/athletes: 1.2-2.0 grams per kg
- Example: A 70kg person needs roughly 56-70 grams daily

**Good protein sources:**
- **Legumes & pulses**: Dal (moong, masoor, toor, chana), rajma, chickpeas, black beans
- **Dairy**: Milk, yogurt (dahi), paneer, cheese
- **Soy products**: Tofu, soy milk, soy chunks
- **Nuts & seeds**: Almonds, peanuts, cashews, pumpkin seeds, chia seeds
- **Eggs**: Whole eggs or egg whites
- **Poultry**: Chicken, turkey
- **Fish**: Salmon, tuna, mackerel, sardines
- **Lean meats**: Mutton (lean cuts)
- **Whole grains**: Quinoa, oats, whole wheat (contain some protein)

**Vegetarian protein combining:**
While not necessary at every meal, combining complementary proteins can enhance amino acid profile:
- Dal with rice
- Roti with dal
- Rajma with rice
- Hummus with whole wheat pita

**Portion guidance:**
- Dal/legumes: 1 cup cooked ≈ 15-18g protein
- Paneer: 100g ≈ 18-20g protein
- Greek yogurt: 1 cup ≈ 15-20g protein
- Chicken breast: 100g ≈ 25-30g protein
- Eggs: 1 large egg ≈ 6g protein
- Almonds: 30g (about 23 almonds) ≈ 6g protein

### Fiber

**What is fiber?**
Fiber is a type of carbohydrate that the body cannot digest. It helps with digestion, blood sugar control, cholesterol management, and promotes feelings of fullness.

**Daily needs:**
- Women: 25 grams
- Men: 38 grams
- Most people consume only 15-20 grams daily

**Types of fiber:**
- **Soluble fiber**: Dissolves in water, helps lower cholesterol and blood sugar (oats, beans, apples, citrus fruits)
- **Insoluble fiber**: Promotes digestive health and regularity (whole wheat, vegetables, nuts, seeds)

**High-fiber foods:**
- **Whole grains**: Oats, brown rice, whole wheat roti/bread, quinoa, barley
- **Legumes**: All dals, rajma, chickpeas, black beans
- **Vegetables**: Broccoli, carrots, beetroot, leafy greens, cauliflower, peas
- **Fruits**: Apples (with skin), pears, berries, guava, oranges, bananas
- **Nuts & seeds**: Almonds, flaxseeds, chia seeds, sunflower seeds

**Fiber content examples:**
- 1 cup cooked dal: 10-15g
- 1 medium apple with skin: 4g
- 1 cup cooked oats: 4g
- 2 whole wheat rotis: 5-6g
- 1 cup cooked brown rice: 3.5g
- 1 cup mixed vegetables: 3-5g

**Tips to increase fiber:**
- Choose whole wheat over refined flour
- Include vegetables at every meal
- Snack on fruits, nuts, or roasted chickpeas
- Add flaxseeds or chia seeds to yogurt or smoothies
- Choose brown rice over white rice (or mix them)
- Include salad before meals

**Important**: Increase fiber gradually and drink plenty of water to prevent digestive discomfort.

### Sugar (Added Sugars)

**Understanding sugars:**
- **Natural sugars**: Found in fruits, milk, and plain yogurt (comes with nutrients and fiber)
- **Added sugars**: Added during processing or preparation (provides calories without nutrients)

**Recommended limits:**
- American Heart Association: <25g (6 tsp) daily for women, <36g (9 tsp) for men
- WHO: <10% of daily calories (ideally <5%)

**Common sources of added sugar:**
- Sweetened beverages (soda, fruit juices, sweetened tea/coffee)
- Desserts and sweets (mithai, ice cream, cakes, cookies)
- Flavored yogurt
- Breakfast cereals
- Sauces and condiments (ketchup, BBQ sauce)
- Packaged snacks

**Hidden sugar names on labels:**
- Sucrose, glucose, fructose
- High fructose corn syrup
- Maltose, dextrose
- Honey, molasses, agave
- Cane sugar, coconut sugar
- Corn sweetener

**Tips to reduce added sugar:**
- Limit sweetened beverages; choose water, unsweetened tea, or nimbu paani
- Use whole fruits instead of fruit juice
- Choose plain yogurt and add fresh fruit
- Gradually reduce sugar in tea/coffee
- Make homemade versions of sweets with less sugar
- Read nutrition labels; choose products with <5g added sugar per serving
- Use spices like cinnamon or cardamom for natural sweetness

**Indian context:**
- Traditional sweets (mithai): High in sugar and ghee; enjoy occasionally in small portions
- Sweetened milk beverages: Opt for plain milk or minimally sweetened versions
- Festival treats: Enjoy mindfully; share portions

### Saturated Fat

**What is saturated fat?**
Fat that is typically solid at room temperature. High intake is associated with increased LDL cholesterol and heart disease risk.

**Recommended limits:**
- <10% of daily calories (for a 2000-calorie diet: <22g daily)
- Ideally <7% for those with heart disease risk

**Main sources:**
- **Ghee and butter**: High in saturated fat
- **Coconut oil and coconut milk**: Despite plant origin, high in saturated fat
- **Palm oil**: Common in processed foods
- **Full-fat dairy**: Whole milk, cream, full-fat cheese, paneer
- **Red meat**: Beef, mutton (especially fatty cuts)
- **Processed meats**: Sausages, bacon
- **Fried foods**: Samosas, pakoras, fried snacks
- **Baked goods**: Pastries, biscuits, cakes

**Healthier alternatives:**
- Use ghee/butter sparingly for flavor; cook with small amounts
- Try mustard oil, rice bran oil, or olive oil for cooking
- Choose low-fat or skim dairy options
- Include more fish, poultry, and plant proteins
- Grill, bake, steam, or sauté instead of deep frying
- When eating fried foods, do so occasionally and in smaller portions

**Balancing traditional cooking:**
- Tadka (tempering): Use small amounts of ghee or oil
- Reduce oil in curries by using tomato puree and onion paste as base
- Use non-stick cookware to reduce oil needs
- Include dry-roasted or lightly sautéed preparations

**Healthy fats to include:**
- **Unsaturated fats**: Olive oil, nuts, seeds, avocado, fatty fish
- **Omega-3s**: Flaxseeds, chia seeds, walnuts, fatty fish (salmon, mackerel)

### Sodium (Salt)

**Why limit sodium?**
Excessive sodium intake raises blood pressure, increases heart disease and stroke risk, and can cause fluid retention.

**Recommended limits:**
- General population: <2,300 mg daily (about 1 teaspoon salt)
- Those with high blood pressure or at risk: <1,500 mg daily
- Average intake in many countries: 3,400+ mg daily

**Main sources:**
- **Table salt**: Used in cooking and at the table
- **Processed foods**: Chips, crackers, packaged snacks
- **Pickles and chutneys**: Very high in sodium
- **Papad and preserved items**
- **Canned foods**: Soups, vegetables, beans (unless labeled low-sodium)
- **Processed meats**: Sausages, cold cuts
- **Restaurant and takeout food**: Often heavily salted
- **Sauces**: Soy sauce, ketchup, chili sauce
- **Cheese and processed cheese**
- **Instant noodles and packaged mixes**

**Tips to reduce sodium:**
- Cook at home more often
- Use less salt in cooking; taste before adding more
- Limit pickles, papad, and heavily salted condiments
- Choose fresh or frozen vegetables over canned
- Rinse canned beans and vegetables
- Read labels; choose products with <140mg sodium per serving (low-sodium)
- Avoid adding salt at the table
- Use herbs and spices for flavor: cumin, coriander, turmeric, ginger, garlic, black pepper, chili powder, curry leaves, mint, cilantro
- Use lemon juice, vinegar, or tamarind for tang instead of salt

**Flavor without excess salt:**
- Fresh herbs and spice blends
- Garlic and ginger paste
- Chaat masala (use low-salt version)
- Black pepper
- Curry leaves for tempering
- Roasted cumin powder
- Fresh green chilies (for heat without salt)

## Indian Dishes: Nutritional Tips & Portion Guidance

### Roti (Chapati)

**Nutritional profile (1 medium whole wheat roti, ~30g):**
- Calories: ~70-80
- Carbohydrates: 15-18g
- Protein: 3g
- Fiber: 2-3g
- Fat: 0.5-1g

**Tips:**
- **Whole wheat** is better than refined flour (maida): higher fiber, more nutrients
- **Multigrain rotis** (with jowar, bajra, ragi) add variety and nutrition
- Avoid excessive ghee or oil when cooking; use minimal for soft texture
- **Thin rotis** cook faster and require less fat
- Make fresh; avoid store-bought rotis with preservatives

**Portion guidance:**
- Sedentary adults: 2-3 medium rotis per meal
- Active individuals: 3-4 rotis per meal
- Pair with dal, sabzi, and protein for balanced meal

**Healthier variations:**
- Mix whole wheat with millet flours
- Add fenugreek leaves (methi) or spinach to dough
- Use water or low-fat milk instead of adding fat to dough

---

### Rice

**Nutritional profile (1 cup cooked white rice, ~200g):**
- Calories: ~200-210
- Carbohydrates: 45g
- Protein: 4g
- Fiber: 0.5-1g
- Fat: 0.5g

**Brown rice (1 cup cooked):**
- Calories: ~215
- Carbohydrates: 45g
- Protein: 5g
- Fiber: 3.5g
- More vitamins and minerals

**Tips:**
- **Brown rice** is more nutritious than white rice: higher fiber, retains bran and germ
- **Mix brown and white rice** if full switch is difficult (start 1:3, gradually increase brown rice proportion)
- **Portion control** is key: rice is calorie-dense
- Avoid fried rice or excessive ghee rice preparations regularly
- **Cool and reheat rice**: Increases resistant starch (slightly better for blood sugar)
- Rinse rice before cooking to remove excess starch

**Portion guidance:**
- Sedentary adults: ½ - ¾ cup cooked rice per meal
- Active individuals: ¾ - 1 cup per meal
- Use smaller bowls to naturally reduce portion size

**Healthier rice dishes:**
- Plain steamed rice with dal and vegetables
- Vegetable pulao (use minimal oil)
- Lemon rice or curd rice (in moderation)
- Mix rice with quinoa or broken wheat

### Dal

**Nutritional profile (1 cup cooked dal):**
- Calories: 180-230 (varies by type and preparation)
- Carbohydrates: 30-40g
- Protein: 15-18g
- Fiber: 10-15g
- Fat: 1-3g (before tempering)

**Types of dal:**
- **Moong dal**: Light, easy to digest, good for all ages
- **Masoor dal**: Quick cooking, high in protein and iron
- **Toor/Arhar dal**: Common in South India, protein-rich
- **Chana dal**: Higher in fiber and protein, slightly heavier
- **Urad dal**: Used in South Indian dishes, calorie-dense

**Tips:**
- **Excellent protein and fiber source**: Pair with rice or roti for complete meal
- Use **minimal oil/ghee** in tempering (tadka); 1-2 tsp is sufficient
- Add **vegetables** (spinach, tomatoes, bottle gourd) to increase nutrients
- Avoid excessive cream or butter in dal makhani-style preparations regularly
- Pressure cooking retains nutrients
- Combine different dals for variety (panchmel dal)

**Portion guidance:**
- 1 cup cooked dal per meal is ideal
- Can increase to 1.5 cups if primary protein source

**Healthier preparations:**
- Simple dal with turmeric, cumin, and tomato
- Dal with spinach (palak dal)
- Sambar (South Indian dal with vegetables)
- Light dal tadka with mustard seeds, cumin, curry leaves
- Avoid frequent cream-based dal preparations

---

### Paneer

**Nutritional profile (100g paneer):**
- Calories: 260-300
- Protein: 18-20g
- Fat: 20-25g (mostly saturated)
- Carbohydrates: 1-3g
- Calcium: ~200mg

**Tips:**
- **Excellent protein source**, especially for vegetarians
- **High in saturated fat and calories**: Use moderate portions
- **Fresh paneer** is better than packaged (fewer preservatives, better texture)
- Avoid deep-fried paneer preparations frequently (paneer pakora, fried paneer in curries)
- Choose **grilled, baked, or lightly sautéed** paneer dishes
- Use **low-fat paneer** if available (reduces saturated fat)

**Portion guidance:**
- 50-75g (about 2-3 cubes) per serving is reasonable
- If primary protein source at meal, can go up to 100g

**Healthier paneer dishes:**
- Paneer tikka (grilled/baked)
- Palak paneer (with minimal cream)
- Paneer bhurji (scrambled with vegetables, use less oil)
- Paneer salad or sandwich
- Cottage cheese (similar to paneer but lower fat)

**Reducing fat:**
- Grill or bake paneer instead of frying
- Use tomato-based gravies instead of cream-based
- Add yogurt for creaminess with less fat
- Increase vegetables in paneer dishes to lower calorie density

---

### Poha

**Nutritional profile (1 cup cooked poha):**
- Calories: 180-250 (depends on preparation)
- Carbohydrates: 35-40g
- Protein: 3-5g
- Fiber: 2-3g
- Fat: 5-10g (depends on oil used)

**Tips:**
- Made from **flattened rice** (beaten rice): Light and easy to digest
- Good **breakfast option**: Quick, satisfying
- Naturally **gluten-free**
- **Add vegetables** (peas, carrots, potatoes, onions) to boost nutrition
- Use **minimal oil** (1-2 tbsp for 2 servings)
- **Add peanuts** for extra protein and healthy fats (small handful)
- Squeeze **lemon juice** before serving: adds vitamin C and enhances iron absorption
- Top with **fresh coriander** for flavor and micronutrients

**Portion guidance:**
- 1-1.5 cups as a breakfast meal
- Pair with yogurt or a glass of milk for additional protein

**Making it healthier:**
- Use less oil; non-stick pan helps
- Load with vegetables (increases fiber and nutrients)
- Add sprouted moong for extra protein
- Use mustard seeds, curry leaves, turmeric for flavor without calories
- Limit added salt
- Avoid deep-fried sev or excessive peanuts as topping

**Variations:**
- Vegetable poha (with mixed vegetables)
- Poha with sprouted moong
- Kanda poha (with onions)
- Batata poha (with potatoes, moderate portion)

### Curd (Yogurt/Dahi)

**Nutritional profile (1 cup plain full-fat dahi, ~240g):**
- Calories: 140-150
- Protein: 8-10g
- Carbohydrates: 11-12g
- Fat: 7-8g
- Calcium: ~300mg
- Probiotics: Beneficial bacteria for gut health

**Low-fat dahi (1 cup):**
- Calories: ~100-110
- Protein: 10-12g
- Fat: 2-3g
- Similar probiotics and calcium

**Tips:**
- **Excellent source of protein, calcium, and probiotics**
- Supports **digestive health** through beneficial bacteria
- **Cooling** properties; good in hot weather
- Choose **plain, unsweetened** curd over flavored/sweetened varieties
- **Low-fat or skim milk curd** reduces saturated fat while keeping protein and calcium
- **Fresh, homemade dahi** is best (no additives or excess sugar)

**Ways to include curd:**
- **Plain**: With meals as a side
- **Raita**: With cucumber, onion, tomato, boondi (use low-fat curd, minimal salt)
- **Buttermilk/Chaas**: Diluted curd with water, cumin, curry leaves (hydrating, probiotic)
- **Lassi**: Plain or with minimal sugar/salt (can be calorie-dense if sweet)
- **Smoothies**: Blend with fruits (natural sweetness)
- **Marinades**: For paneer or chicken (tenderizes, adds flavor)
- **Kadhi**: Yogurt-based curry (use less oil, more vegetables)

**Portion guidance:**
- ½ - 1 cup per day is beneficial
- Can have more if it's primary dairy source

**Healthier preparations:**
- Plain dahi with meals
- Cucumber raita (minimal salt)
- Chaas/buttermilk (unsweetened or lightly salted)
- Fruit yogurt (add your own fresh fruit to plain curd)
- Avoid excessive sugar in lassi or shrikhand
- Choose low-fat curd for weight management

**Cautions:**
- If lactose intolerant, may still tolerate curd better than milk (lower lactose, probiotics aid digestion)
- Avoid if dairy allergy; choose plant-based yogurt alternatives

## Portion Control Strategies

### Visual portion guides:
- **Protein** (meat, fish, paneer, tofu): Palm of hand or deck of cards (~75-100g)
- **Grains** (rice, roti): Cupped hand or fist (~½-1 cup cooked)
- **Vegetables**: Two cupped hands (at least 1-2 cups)
- **Fats** (oil, ghee, nuts): Thumb tip (~1 tsp) or thumb (~1 tbsp)
- **Snacks**: Small handful (~30g nuts)

### Plate method (balanced meal):
- **½ plate**: Vegetables (non-starchy)
- **¼ plate**: Protein (dal, paneer, chicken, fish, eggs)
- **¼ plate**: Whole grains (roti, brown rice)
- **Side**: Small serving of curd or buttermilk

### Practical tips:
- Use smaller plates and bowls
- Serve food in kitchen, not at table (reduces mindless seconds)
- Eat slowly and mindfully
- Start meals with salad or soup
- Don't skip meals (leads to overeating later)
- Listen to hunger and fullness cues
- Avoid distractions while eating (TV, phone)

## Meal Planning Tips

### Balanced meal structure:
- **Breakfast**: Whole grain + protein + fruit
  - Example: Poha with peanuts and vegetables + banana
  - Example: Oats with milk, nuts, and berries
  - Example: Whole wheat paratha with curd and fruit

- **Lunch/Dinner**: Grain + protein + vegetables + healthy fat
  - Example: Roti + dal + sabzi + small salad with lemon
  - Example: Brown rice + rajma + stir-fried vegetables + raita
  - Example: Quinoa + paneer tikka + mixed vegetables

- **Snacks**: Combine protein/fat with carbs for satisfaction
  - Fruits with nuts
  - Curd with cucumber
  - Whole grain crackers with hummus
  - Roasted chickpeas (chana)
  - Vegetable sticks with yogurt dip

### Weekly variety:
- Rotate different dals (moong, masoor, toor, chana)
- Include fish 2-3 times/week if non-vegetarian
- Try different vegetables each week
- Alternate between rice and roti
- Include eggs several times/week if consumed

### Batch cooking & prep:
- Cook dal and rice in larger batches
- Pre-chop vegetables for quick cooking
- Prepare roti dough in advance
- Keep frozen vegetables for convenience
- Make homemade masala mixes to reduce salt in store-bought

## Smart Substitutions

### For healthier cooking:
- **Instead of deep-frying**: Bake, air-fry, grill, or shallow fry with minimal oil
- **Instead of white flour (maida)**: Whole wheat flour, multigrain flour
- **Instead of white rice**: Brown rice, quinoa, or brown/white rice mix
- **Instead of full-fat dairy**: Low-fat or skim options
- **Instead of cream**: Hung curd (Greek yogurt style), cashew paste
- **Instead of excess oil/ghee**: Use minimal amounts; use spray oil; use non-stick cookware
- **Instead of store-bought sauces**: Make fresh chutneys and sauces at home
- **Instead of refined oil**: Mustard oil, olive oil, rice bran oil (in moderation)

### For reducing calories:
- Use vegetable purees to thicken gravies instead of cream
- Replace half the paneer in dishes with tofu
- Bulk up meals with extra vegetables
- Use spices and herbs for flavor instead of excess fat and salt
- Choose clear soups over creamy soups
- Opt for grilled/tandoori over fried options

## Grocery Shopping Tips

### Read labels:
- **Ingredients list**: Shorter is usually better; recognize all ingredients
- **Serving size**: Check actual serving size vs. package contents
- **Protein**: Aim for at least 3g per serving for snacks
- **Fiber**: Look for 3g+ per serving
- **Added sugars**: Choose <5g per serving
- **Sodium**: Choose <140mg per serving (low-sodium)
- **Saturated fat**: Lower is better

### Shop smart:
- **Perimeter of store**: Fresh produce, dairy, eggs, fresh proteins
- **Avoid center aisles** with heavily processed foods (or be selective)
- **Bulk bins**: Nuts, seeds, whole grains, dried legumes (often cheaper)
- **Frozen section**: Plain frozen vegetables and fruits (no sauce/sugar added)
- **Shop with a list**: Reduces impulse purchases
- **Don't shop hungry**: Leads to less healthy choices

### Staples to stock:
- Whole grains: Brown rice, oats, quinoa, whole wheat flour
- Pulses/legumes: Variety of dals, chickpeas, rajma
- Frozen vegetables: For convenience
- Nuts and seeds: Almonds, walnuts, pumpkin seeds, chia, flax
- Healthy oils: Olive oil, mustard oil
- Spices and herbs: Full range for flavorful cooking
- Low-fat dairy or alternatives
- Eggs (if consumed)
- Fresh produce: Buy what you'll use within a week

## Mindful Eating Practices

### Eat with awareness:
- Sit down for meals (avoid eating while standing/walking)
- Minimize distractions (turn off TV, put away phone)
- Eat slowly; chew thoroughly (20-30 chews per bite)
- Notice flavors, textures, and aromas
- Pause between bites
- Check in with hunger and fullness levels

### Hunger vs. fullness scale (1-10):
- 1-2: Ravenous, dizzy, irritable
- 3-4: Hungry, ready to eat
- 5-6: Satisfied, comfortable
- 7-8: Full, slightly uncomfortable
- 9-10: Stuffed, very uncomfortable

**Aim to**: Start eating at 3-4, stop at 6-7

### Emotional eating:
- Identify triggers (stress, boredom, emotions)
- Find non-food coping strategies (walk, call friend, journal)
- Keep tempting foods out of immediate reach
- Practice self-compassion (one meal doesn't define health)

## Special Considerations

### Weight management:
- Create modest calorie deficit through combination of diet and activity
- Focus on whole, minimally processed foods
- Include adequate protein (promotes satiety)
- Eat plenty of vegetables (high volume, low calories)
- Control portions of calorie-dense foods (oils, nuts, sweets)
- Stay hydrated
- Be patient; sustainable loss is 0.5-1kg per week

### Meal timing:
- Eat breakfast within 1-2 hours of waking
- Space meals 3-5 hours apart
- Include protein at each meal for sustained energy
- Finish dinner 2-3 hours before bed
- Consistency helps regulate hunger hormones

### Dining out:
- Check menu online beforehand
- Choose grilled, baked, or steamed options
- Ask for sauces/dressings on the side
- Start with salad or clear soup
- Share dishes or take half home
- Don't arrive overly hungry
- Enjoy without guilt; balance with other meals

---

**Final Note**: Nutrition is not one-size-fits-all. These guidelines provide a general framework for healthy eating. Individual needs vary based on age, activity level, health conditions, and personal goals. For personalized nutrition advice, consult a registered dietitian or nutritionist. Focus on progress, not perfection, and remember that overall eating patterns matter more than individual meals.
